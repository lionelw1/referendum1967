Understanding the events that lead to the 1967 Referendum

Before the Referendum

The years after federation lead to great growth for Australia as a nation. Most Australians enjoyed the rights and freedoms granted by the new government and her constitution, but even since European settlement, the Indigenous peoples of Australia have suffered from the paternalistic views of the Europeans, which, under federal government, lead to policies of assimilation and segregation. This protectionist attitude, was deeply rooted in racism and in the ideology that Indigennous Australians were somehow inferior to Europeans. 

Maralinga Atomic Bomb Tests

1957

Maralinga was inhabited by the Pitjantjatjara and Yankunytjatjara Aboriginals, and was of great cultural significance to them. Between 1956 and 1957, 7 tests were conducted, yeilding a colossal total 31.6 kilotonnes of TNT.
The resulting nuclear fallout contaminated much of the Maralinga site and attracted scathing media attention, especially to the Aboriginals that contracted radiation poisoning or forced off their land.

Commonwealth Electoral Act

1962

The act enabled Indigenous Australians to enrol and vote in federal elections, but enrolment was not compulsory. However, it was still illegal under Commonwealth legislation to encourage Indigenous Australians to vote. In the same year, the Northern Territory and Western Australia gave the right to vote in state elections to Indigenous Australians. Queensland was the last to allow Aboriginal people to vote in state elections in 1965.

Wave Hill Strike

1966

In the August of 1964, Gurindji Aboriginal workers walked off the vast Wave Hill pastoral station in the Northern Territory, in protest for equal wages. The group, led by Vincent Lingiari, camped some 30km from Wave Hill Station at Wattie Creek, near a site of cultural significance. They set up a sign (pictured) declaring their claim to traditional land. This transformed the strike into a land claim for traditional Aboriginal land, the first of its kind and made headlines across Australia.

Freedom Rides

1966

The so-called 'Freedom Rides' was a bus tour that  travelled through northern New South Wales towns to survey Aboriginal living conditions and rights, and protest against discrimination. The protests exposed an endemic racism throughout NSW, which shocked the far less racist city-dwellers and put pressure on politicians for change.

1967

Catalyst for change
A referendum to change sections 51 and 127 was proposed as early as 1962. In the two years afterward, more than 50 petitions were submitted until, in 1965, a referendum was announced to take place during May the following year. However, after the 1966 federal election, the new Prime Minister Harold Holt postponed the referendum to 1967, because if the referendum failed, as many had in the past, it would be seen as a setback to the new government. As a result, the date for the referendum was set to 27th of May, 1967.

The vote

The votes were tallied and were overwhelmingly in favour of passing the referendum. 91% of the population voted 'Yes' and only 9% voted 'no', the single most popular referendum to be carried in Australian history to date.

The Outcome

What did the 1967 Referendum change for Aboriginal Australians?

What the Referendum actually achieved seems insignificant. The 'Yes' vote meant that Sections 51 and 127 of the Constitution were ammended. Before the ammendment, the Constitution specifically excluded Aborigines from being counted as Australians in the census, and barred the Commonwealth from creating laws to benefit the Aboriginal population. The referendum changed relatively little, but its massive majority vote came as a milestone for the civil rights movement and marked the climax of a decade long battle for equality for Aboriginals. 

In the eyes of the Aboriginal community, the referendum allowed them to feel accepted, as members of a united nation and counted as members of that nation. Thegovernment could now pass legislature expressly for the benefit of the Aboriginal people. Although these two small changes seem insignificant, they had changed discriminating laws that had been in place for all of Australia's brief history and can be seen as a turning point for civil rights movement. From 1967 onwards, the movement gained traction and achieved Aboriginal land rights and in 1976, and the terra nullius decision was overturned in the landmark Mabo vs State of Queensland case. 

Vincent Lingiari

Vincent Lingiari, mentioned above was the leader of the Wave Hill walk-off. As a direct result of this he sparked the first case for Aboriginal land rights in Australian history. 
In March 1973, his dream was realised .
The Gurundji people were granted 3236 square kilometres of cultural land. Then Prime Minister Gough Whitlam poured red sand into Lingiari's hands in a televised ceremony in a symbolic guesture.
Lingiari went on to recieve the Member of  the Order of Australia for his service to Aboriginal people.
His fight for equal rights made him a national figure.

Celebrating Unity, 
Remembering Change

The result of the 1967 Referendum, for most Australians, would have little significance or relevance today. However we must stop to remember our past - the decisions that made our history, shape our future.
This is why, with the allocation of government funding to go toward raising awareness of the history of Indigenous civil rights movement, we, the IHCA, need your support to remember this milestone in the movement. All we need is your email and a minute of your time, to help remember change and celebrate unity.
